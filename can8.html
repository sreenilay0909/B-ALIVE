Liver Cancer Overview

Liver cancer, also known as hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), is a type of cancer that originates in the liver. It is one of the most common cancers worldwide and is often associated with chronic liver diseases, such as hepatitis and cirrhosis.

Types of Liver Cancer

  • Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC): The most prevalent form, accounting for about 75-85% of liver cancer cases. It usually arises from liver cells (hepatocytes).
  • Cholangiocarcinoma: Also known as bile duct cancer, this type originates in the bile ducts and can occur within the liver or outside it.
  • Hepatoblastoma: A rare type of liver cancer that primarily affects children, typically under the age of three.
  • Angiosarcoma: A rare cancer that originates in the blood vessels of the liver.

Risk Factors

  • Chronic Viral Hepatitis: Infection with hepatitis B (HBV) or hepatitis C (HCV) viruses significantly increases risk.
  • Cirrhosis: Scarring of the liver from chronic liver disease, which can result from alcohol abuse, hepatitis, fatty liver disease, or other causes.
  • Alcohol Consumption: Chronic heavy drinking can lead to liver damage and increase cancer risk.
  • Obesity and Diabetes: These conditions are linked to fatty liver disease, which can progress to cirrhosis and increase cancer risk.
  • Aflatoxin Exposure: Contamination of food products (especially grains and nuts) with aflatoxin, a carcinogenic substance produced by certain molds, is a risk factor in some regions.
  • Family History: A family history of liver disease or liver cancer can increase risk.

Symptoms

Early-stage liver cancer often does not cause symptoms. As the disease progresses, symptoms may include:

  • Unexplained weight loss.
  • Loss of appetite.
  • Upper abdominal pain or discomfort.
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Fatigue and weakness.
  • Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes).
  • Swelling in the abdomen (ascites).

Diagnosis

Diagnosis typically involves several steps:

  • Medical History and Physical Examination: A healthcare provider will assess symptoms and may check for liver enlargement or tenderness.
  • Imaging Tests: Techniques such as ultrasound, CT scans, or MRIs can help visualize the liver and detect tumors.
  • Blood Tests: Tests for liver function and tumor markers, such as alpha-fetoprotein (AFP), which can be elevated in liver cancer.
  • Biopsy: A tissue sample may be taken from the liver for examination to confirm cancer diagnosis.

Treatment

Treatment for liver cancer depends on various factors, including the size and number of tumors, liver function, and the overall health of the patient. Common treatment options include:

  • Surgery: If the cancer is confined to a portion of the liver, surgical options may include partial hepatectomy (removal of part of the liver) or liver transplantation.
  • Ablation: Techniques such as radiofrequency ablation (RFA) or microwave ablation destroy tumors using heat.
  • Transarterial Chemoembolization (TACE): A procedure that delivers chemotherapy directly to the tumor while blocking its blood supply.
  • Radiation Therapy: May be used to shrink tumors or relieve symptoms, though it’s less common for liver cancer.
  • Targeted Therapy: Drugs that target specific molecules involved in cancer growth, such as sorafenib or lenvatinib, may be used for advanced cases.
  • Immunotherapy: This approach helps the body’s immune system recognize and attack cancer cells and may be effective for certain patients.

Prevention

Preventive measures can help reduce the risk of liver cancer:

  • Vaccination: Hepatitis B vaccination can significantly lower the risk of HBV-related liver cancer.
  • Regular Screening: Individuals at high risk (e.g., those with chronic hepatitis or cirrhosis) should undergo regular screenings for liver cancer.
  • Limit Alcohol Consumption: Reducing alcohol intake can help protect liver health.
  • Maintain a Healthy Weight: Managing weight through diet and exercise can reduce the risk of fatty liver disease and associated complications.
  • Avoid Aflatoxin: Ensuring food safety and proper storage can minimize exposure to aflatoxin.
